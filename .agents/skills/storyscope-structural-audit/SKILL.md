# StoryScope Structural Audit

## Core Idea

Use this before sentence polish when a draft needs to feel less default, less machine-shaped, or more creatively reasoned. The method comes from StoryScope-style narrative-feature thinking: AI-like writing often fails at structure before it fails at wording.

Do not use this as proof of authorship. Treat it as a craft and thinking checklist, not a detector or legal claim.

## Fast Workflow

1. Identify the artifact type: fiction, essay, email, assignment, PRD, feature idea, journey map, PR/FAQ, or memo.
2. Separate surface style from structure.
3. Run the anti-default checks below.
4. Revise the structure first.
5. Only then polish wording, tone, rhythm, and formatting.

## Anti-Default Checks

Flag the draft when it:

- explains the lesson instead of letting the reader infer it
- creates a single clean causal chain with no friction, detour, or competing pressure
- resolves through neat internal acceptance when external action, tradeoff, or ambiguity would be truer
- uses generic emotion-through-body cues instead of specific behavior, choice, dialogue, or consequence
- avoids named evidence, real references, concrete objects, specific places, or actual constraints
- treats the audience as passive instead of anticipating questions, objections, confusion, or reader knowledge gaps
- has no meaningful subplot, counterpoint, secondary stakeholder, or parallel pressure
- moves in a straight line when delayed disclosure, sequence reversal, or before/after contrast would improve understanding
- makes the protagonist, user, customer, or decision-maker too morally clean
- ends with a tidy conclusion that hides unresolved risk, cost, ownership, or next decision

## Revision Moves

Choose only moves that fit the artifact.

- Replace a stated moral with a concrete consequence.
- Add one competing incentive or constraint.
- Add one named reference, source, customer quote, location, artifact, metric, or operational detail.
- Change a linear sequence into: current pain -> failed workaround -> trigger -> decision point -> consequence.
- Add a second stakeholder whose incentives create tension.
- Move one important fact later if revelation improves the reader's understanding.
- Make the ending honest: decision, tradeoff, residual risk, owner, next step.

## By Artifact

### Fiction

Check theme, agency, subplot, time, revelation, moral ambiguity, reader address, and specificity. Prefer structural fixes before voice edits.

### Essays And Assignments

Check whether the argument has named evidence, a real counterargument, and one unresolved tension. Remove meta-framing about the assignment unless the user explicitly asks for it.

### Emails

Check whether the message has a clear ask, a real constraint, concrete context, and the reader's likely objection. Do not add literary complexity to routine emails.

### PRDs And Features

Treat the user as a protagonist with pressure, limited knowledge, workarounds, constraints, and decision points. A strong PRD should show the situation before the feature, the trigger for use, the tradeoff the user faces, and what changes after success.

### Strategy And Decision Memos

Avoid polished inevitability. Show rejected options, weak evidence, competing incentives, residual risk, and the exact decision needed.

## Output Pattern

For audits, lead with the structural verdict:

```markdown
## Structural Verdict
[Direct answer: what feels default or strong.]

## Main Structural Problems
- [Problem] -> [Why it weakens the draft] -> [Specific fix]

## Revision Plan
1. [Highest-impact structural change]
2. [Second change]
3. [Final polish step]

## Optional Rewrite
[Only include if the user asked for rewriting.]
```

For creation tasks, use this as an internal pre-flight and produce the requested artifact directly.
